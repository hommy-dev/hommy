# Design Context — RoofLink

## Users

**Two distinct audiences with opposite trust postures:**

- **Roofing contractors (supply side).** Solo to mid-size roofing businesses in the US. Every one of them has been burned by Angi, HomeAdvisor, Thumbtack, or Bark — paying $100–300 for a "lead" that got blasted to 5 competitors at once. 72% of them run on no CRM at all: spreadsheets, sticky notes, and memory. They read marketing copy with active suspicion. The platform's job is to dismantle that skepticism with concrete promises, not feel-good language.
- **Homeowners (demand side).** They have a roof problem — a leak, storm damage, an aging roof, or an insurance inspection — and they want it handled. Anxious about being scammed or overcharged. They want speed (a real contractor calling back fast), proof (licensed, insured, reviewed pros), and zero obligation.

**Job to be done.**
- Contractors: get exclusive, high-intent leads that actually convert — never shared, never auctioned — plus a mobile CRM built for how they actually work (in a truck, on a roof, between jobs).
- Homeowners: get matched to one vetted local roofer who calls back within the hour, with no account to create and no commitment.

## Brand Personality

**Three words:** *Honest. Plain-spoken. Slightly-opinionated.*

The brand voice is a roofer who learned bookkeeping. Direct, unvarnished, occasionally pissed off about the way lead-gen platforms have treated contractors for the last decade. Not corporate. Not "modern." Not "elevated." Closer to a small-batch coffee roaster's about page than a SaaS hero.

**The promise that wraps everything:** *"One lead. One contractor. Never shared."*

Every page, every email, every CTA must reinforce this single idea — exclusivity and speed. Shared leads are the enemy we're named against. If a piece of copy or a design choice doesn't land the "your lead, yours alone" point, cut it.

**Emotional goals:**
- Contractors should feel: *"finally, a lead that's actually mine — and a tool that doesn't fight me."*
- Homeowners should feel: *"someone real will call me back fast, and these people are vetted pros."*

## Aesthetic Direction

**Editorial / chalk-line.** Closer to a trade publication or magazine pull-quote than a SaaS landing page. Big honest numbers in high-contrast display serif, generous body prose, asymmetric sections, accent stripes that read like a snapped chalk line across a roof deck used as a graphic motif (not as side-stripe card borders — those are banned).

**Anti-references** (we explicitly do NOT look like these):
- Angi, HomeAdvisor, Thumbtack, Bark — all generic, all dashboard-y, all visually forgettable, all built on the shared-lead model we reject.
- Generic SaaS landing pages with hero metric cards, gradient text, glassmorphism, identical 3-column feature grids.
- Anything that screams "AI-generated."

**Theme: Light.** Warm cream paper background — `oklch(0.985 0.005 90)` — not pure white. Read in daylight by homeowners on phones and contractors on tablets in their truck. Light context.

**Color palette** (inherits existing tokens — they're already distinctive enough):
- Primary: deep blue `#0055a4` — the ink color, used for body text and primary buttons.
- Secondary: lime `#dcf4a2` — used sparingly as a chalk-line / highlight accent.
- Tertiary: pink `#ff7aa2` — used even more sparingly, for emphasis spots only.
- Neutrals tinted slightly toward the deep blue.

**Typography:**
- **Bricolage Grotesque** (Google Fonts, variable) — display + UI sans. Has parametric width/weight axes, characterful enough to escape the "Inter monoculture."
- **Spectral** italic (Google Fonts, already loaded) — narrative / pull-quote voice.
- **Gloock** (Google Fonts) — Bodoni-style high-contrast serif, used only for hero numbers (the "78%", "8 hrs", "$0", etc.) — magazine pull-quote energy.

**Banned fonts** (explicit reflex-list, do not use any of these): Inter, Fraunces, Newsreader, Lora, Crimson, Playfair, Cormorant, Syne, IBM Plex, Space Mono, Space Grotesk, DM Sans, DM Serif, Outfit, Plus Jakarta, Instrument.

## Design Principles

1. **Exclusivity is the design.** Every section should land the "one lead, one contractor, never shared" idea from a different angle. The contrast against shared-lead platforms is the brand identity, not a footnote.
2. **Editorial rhythm, not feature grids.** Mix long-form prose with big numerical pull quotes. Vary section spacing dramatically. Avoid the 3-column "feature card" template at all costs.
3. **Numbers do the talking.** When making a claim, lead with a number — "78% hire the first to respond", "8+ hour average response time", "$0 shared leads", "5–7 follow-ups to close". Numbers in display serif. Words in body sans.
4. **Concrete, not aspirational.** Replace any sentence that sounds like a brochure with one that has a number or a specific behavior. "Fast" → "a contractor calls within 60 minutes." "Affordable" → "$99/mo, 5 exclusive leads."
5. **Voice over visuals.** This brand is carried by writing, not chrome. When in doubt, cut a graphic and add a paragraph.
6. **Asymmetric layouts.** Off-center, varied widths, sections that don't all look the same. Centered hero stacks are forbidden after the first one.
7. **No card-soup.** Don't wrap everything in cards. Most content lives directly on the page.

## Implementation Notes

- Tailwind v4, Next.js 16 (App Router, cacheComponents), shadcn/ui base.
- Existing CSS variables in `src/app/globals.css` — keep, don't rewrite. Override at component level when a section needs the cream-paper background.
- Framer Motion already installed for entrance animations.
- Pricing tiers and platform tunables (plan limits, lead expiry, review delay — see `docs/ROOFING_PLATFORM.md` §7–§10) live in a dedicated config module, never hard-coded into pages.
